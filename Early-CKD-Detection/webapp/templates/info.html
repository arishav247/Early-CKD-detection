What are Kidneys?

The kidneys are two bean-shaped organs, each about the size of a fist, located just below the rib cage on either side of the spine. They are vital organs that perform several crucial functions to keep the body healthy:

  • Filter waste products from the blood
  • Remove excess water and electrolytes
  • Help regulate blood pressure
  • Produce hormones that affect other organs
  • Maintain acid-base balance
Did you know? Your kidneys filter about 200 liters of blood every day!

How Kidneys Work

The kidneys perform their filtering function through tiny structures called nephrons. Each kidney contains about one million nephrons.

1

Filtration

Blood enters the kidneys through the renal artery and passes through millions of tiny filters called glomeruli.

2

Reabsorption

Useful substances like water, glucose, and amino acids are reabsorbed back into the bloodstream.

3

Secretion

Additional waste products are actively secreted from the blood into the forming urine.

4

Excretion

The final urine passes through the ureters to the bladder for storage and eventual elimination.

Common Kidney Diseases

Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD)

Gradual loss of kidney function over time. Often caused by diabetes or high blood pressure.

Symptoms:

  • Fatigue
  • Swelling in legs
  • Changes in urination

Acute Kidney Injury

Sudden episode of kidney failure or damage that happens within a few hours or days.

Symptoms:

  • Decreased urine output
  • Fluid retention
  • Confusion

Kidney Stones

Hard deposits of minerals and salts that form inside your kidneys.

Symptoms:

  • Severe pain in side and back
  • Painful urination
  • Pink, red or brown urine

Kidney Infections

A type of urinary tract infection that begins in the urethra or bladder and travels to the kidneys.

Symptoms:

  • Fever
  • Back or side pain
  • Frequent urination

Important Kidney Parameters

Creatinine

0.6 - 1.2 mg/dL (Men: 0.7-1.3, Women: 0.5-1.1)

Waste product from muscle metabolism. Higher levels may indicate kidney dysfunction.

Levels can be affected by muscle mass and diet.

eGFR

≥ 90 mL/min/1.73m² (Varies with age)

Estimated Glomerular Filtration Rate measures how well kidneys filter blood.

Below 60 for 3+ months indicates CKD.

Blood Urea Nitrogen (BUN)

7 - 20 mg/dL

Measures nitrogen in blood from urea waste product.

Can be affected by dehydration and high protein diet.

Urine Albumin

< 30 mg/g creatinine

Measures protein in urine (albuminuria).

Early sign of kidney damage, especially in diabetes.

Complete Kidney Function Test Reference Ranges

Parameter Normal Range Significance
Serum Creatinine 0.6-1.2 mg/dL Kidney filtration marker
BUN 7-20 mg/dL Nitrogen waste product
eGFR >90 mL/min/1.73m² Kidney function level
Sodium (Na+) 135-145 mmol/L Electrolyte balance
Potassium (K+) 3.5-5.0 mmol/L Critical for heart function

Kidney Disease Prevention

Stay Hydrated

Drink adequate water (about 2 liters daily) to help kidneys clear sodium and toxins.

Eat Healthy

Reduce sodium, processed foods, and maintain a balanced diet with fresh fruits and vegetables.

Monitor Blood Pressure

Keep BP below 140/90 mmHg. High BP is a leading cause of kidney damage.

Control Blood Sugar

Diabetes is the #1 cause of kidney failure. Manage your glucose levels carefully.

Avoid Smoking

Smoking reduces blood flow to kidneys and worsens existing kidney disease.

Use Medications Wisely

Some OTC pain medications can harm kidneys if taken regularly.
